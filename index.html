Research

How do you weigh the magnetic field of a star you cannot resolve? You read it off the light.

Polars (AM Her stars)

Cataclysmic variables in which the white-dwarf field is strong enough (10–240 MG) to lock the binary into synchronous rotation and suppress the accretion disk entirely. Gas free-falls along field lines onto the magnetic pole.

Cyclotron spectroscopy

At the accretion shock, hot electrons gyrate and radiate at harmonics of the cyclotron frequency, νc = eB/2πmec. The spacing of the resulting humps is a direct, model-light field measurement.

Automated spectral fitting

I fit a Koester white-dwarf photosphere, a hot-spot blackbody, and an isothermal constant-Λ cyclotron component simultaneously, using profiled χ²(B) curves to expose competing harmonic assignments instead of hiding them.

Time-domain follow-up

I mine 6–7 yr of ZTF photometry for orbital periods and aliases — finding, for instance, a rare short-period eclipsing-polar candidate below the period gap.

Machine learning on light curves

CNN and SVM classifiers — with engineered period and shape features and DBSCAN clustering — sift ~10⁸ ZTF light curves to surface rare variables: magnetic CVs, eclipsing systems and other oddities, pulled out of a survey-scale haystack into a short, vettable candidate list.

Disk-eclipsing binaries & clusters

Flat-bottomed “square-wave” eclipses in which a misaligned circumbinary disk occults a low-mass binary (à la KH 15D). Open-cluster membership turns these into age-dated probes of long-lived circumstellar dust.

AI agents for astronomy

I build LLM-agent pipelines that wrap data retrieval, modelling and evidence-auditing into a reviewable graph — RAG + knowledge graphs, tool-calling, and domain-tuned models that refuse to publish a claim the evidence doesn't support.

Square-wave eclipse: a disk-eclipsing binary

UPK 13-c2 dims in a flat-bottomed “square wave” every 36.71 days. A misaligned circumbinary disk's localized edge occults just one component of a late-K/early-M binary; that star carries ~40% of the light, so the flux drops to ~60% while the companion keeps shining. It's a real 3-D model of the disk + binary: drag the viewing angle from edge-on toward face-on and the ring opens up — the eclipse only happens when we see it near edge-on. The slow, multi-day ingress is the clincher — switch the occulted source to a white dwarf and watch the walls snap vertical, which is exactly why a WD is ruled out.

ZTF light-curve classifier — a lightweight CNN

A different way to mine the same surveys: turn every phase-folded ZTF light curve into a 224×224 grayscale image, then let a mobile-grade convolutional net read its shape. It sorts each star into EA (detached, Algol-type), EW (contact, W UMa-type) or Non-EB — reaching 99.4–99.6% on 1,884 held-out curves. Pick a class and watch the forward pass; drag the photometric noise up and the softmax loses its nerve. Code: ztf_CNN_maoclassification.

Backbone

The pipeline is real: ZTF g/r photometry pulled from IRSA, a Lomb–Scargle period, phase-folding, then a 1×224×224 render fed to GhostNet or MobileNetV2. Accuracies, parameter counts and the confusion matrix are the repository's reported values; the forward-pass animation is schematic.

Light curve → image

Each star's irregular time series is folded on its Lomb–Scargle period and rendered as a 224×224 grayscale frame. Casting the signal as a picture lets mature vision backbones read eclipse morphology directly — depth asymmetry, flat vs. continuous minima.

Lightweight by design

GhostNet (5.2M params) and MobileNetV2 (3.5M) are mobile-grade nets — single-channel input, ~141–300M FLOPs. Small enough to sweep survey-scale catalogues on modest hardware instead of a GPU farm.

Built for imbalance

Real surveys are mostly junk: the test split holds 124 EA and 418 EW against 1,342 Non-EB. AdamW, cosine annealing, label smoothing (ε=0.1) and rotation/flip augmentation keep the rare classes from being drowned out.

Honest metrics

Beyond 99.4–99.6% accuracy, I report per-class precision/recall and the full confusion matrix — so the weak spot is visible: detached EA systems, the scarcest class, sit at 0.78 recall and sometimes read as EW.

Astro Agent — an auditable research agent

My open-source AI project (github.com/wangnengdejiamao/Astro_Agent): a LangGraph state machine that resolves a target, pulls multi-survey data, models it through three mandatory iterations, audits every claim against the evidence, and — only when the QA gate clears — drafts an ApJ-style manuscript with peer-review notes. Two cooperating layers

A LangGraph analysis_agent orchestrates the workflow; an astro_toolbox of 20+ survey clients and modelling modules (SED, white-dwarf fitting, RV/period, kinematic traceback, cluster membership) does the science. They talk only through structured JSON.

Retrieval built for jargon

BM25 with a domain pre-tagger (46 instruments × 24 method families) beats generic dense retrieval on method-transfer queries like Lomb–Scargle or Bayestar2019 — cheap, auditable, and shared by the RAG navigator and method scout.

Evidence-gated claims

A per-target evidence pack lists which parameter claims are supported and which are blocked pending evidence. With no spectra, the agent reports provisional Teff/radius from SED+Gaia but blocks spectral type, log g, mass and composition.

PaperOrchestra drafting

Manuscript writing is split into five deterministic sub-agents — outline, plotting, literature review, section writing, refinement — producing aastex631 LaTeX that a QC checklist and a bounded reflexion loop hold to ApJ standards.
